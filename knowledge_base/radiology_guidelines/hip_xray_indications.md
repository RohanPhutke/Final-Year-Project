# Hip X-Ray — Indications & Assessment Guidelines

## When to Request Hip X-Ray After a Fall

### Absolute Indications
- Inability to weight-bear (4 steps, immediately and in the emergency department)
- Shortened and externally rotated limb
- Tenderness on palpation of the greater trochanter
- Tenderness on axial loading of the limb
- Any lateral fall with high-energy impact in patients aged > 50

### Relative Indications
- Groin pain after fall, even if weight-bearing
- Anticoagulated patient with hip pain after fall
- Known osteoporosis with any mechanism of fall
- Previous contralateral hip fracture

## Standard Views
1. **AP Pelvis**: Both hips visible for comparison, 15° internal rotation of affected leg (if tolerated)
2. **Lateral of affected hip**: Cross-table lateral (Danelius-Miller) — patient supine, unaffected leg flexed

## What to Look For
- **Femoral neck fracture**: Disruption of Shenton's line, cortical break at the femoral neck
- **Intertrochanteric fracture**: Fracture line between greater and lesser trochanters
- **Subtrochanteric fracture**: Fracture below the lesser trochanter
- **Pubic rami fracture**: Often concurrent with sacral insufficiency fracture
- **Acetabular fracture**: Disruption of iliopectineal or ilioischial lines

## Occult Fractures
- Up to 10% of hip fractures are NOT visible on initial X-ray
- If clinical suspicion remains despite negative X-ray:
  - **MRI** (gold standard): sensitivity >99%, within 24 hours
  - **CT scan**: good alternative if MRI unavailable, sensitivity ~93%
  - **Repeat X-ray** at 7–10 days: NOT recommended as sole follow-up (delayed diagnosis is harmful)

## Classification Systems
### Garden Classification (Femoral Neck Fractures)
- **Type I**: Incomplete/impacted (valgus)
- **Type II**: Complete but non-displaced
- **Type III**: Complete, partially displaced
- **Type IV**: Complete, fully displaced

### AO/OTA Classification
- 31-A: Trochanteric
- 31-B: Femoral neck
- 31-C: Head

## Urgency
- **STAT**: If hemodynamically unstable or concurrent injuries requiring surgical planning
- **URGENT** (within 1 hour): Standard presentation — pain, inability to weight-bear
- **ROUTINE**: Ambulatory patient with mild pain — less likely fracture but still warrants imaging
