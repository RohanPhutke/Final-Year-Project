# CT Head — Canadian CT Head Rule & Imaging Guidelines

## Overview
The Canadian CT Head Rule (CCHR) provides evidence-based criteria for ordering CT scans in patients with minor head injuries (GCS 13–15). It has a sensitivity approaching 100% for neurosurgical intervention and 98.4% for clinically important brain injury.

## Canadian CT Head Rule

### Applicability
The rule applies to patients with:
- GCS 13–15
- Witnessed loss of consciousness, definite amnesia, or witnessed disorientation
- Age ≥ 16 years

### Exclusion Criteria (Rule does NOT apply)
- Non-trauma cases
- GCS < 13
- Anticoagulant use or known bleeding disorder
- Obvious open or depressed skull fracture

### HIGH-RISK Criteria (for neurosurgical intervention)
CT is required if ANY of the following are present:
1. GCS < 15 at 2 hours after injury
2. Suspected open or depressed skull fracture
3. Any sign of basal skull fracture:
   - Hemotympanum
   - Raccoon eyes (periorbital ecchymosis)
   - Battle sign (mastoid ecchymosis)
   - CSF otorrhea or rhinorrhea
4. Two or more episodes of vomiting
5. Age ≥ 65 years

### MEDIUM-RISK Criteria (for brain injury on CT)
CT is required if ANY of the following are present:
6. Amnesia before impact > 30 minutes
7. Dangerous mechanism:
   - Pedestrian struck by motor vehicle
   - Occupant ejected from motor vehicle
   - **Fall from height > 3 feet or > 5 stairs**

## Application to Fall Detection Context
For fall detection systems:
- **Age ≥ 65**: This criterion alone mandates CT in any head-injured elderly faller with LOC/amnesia/disorientation
- **Fall from standing height in elderly**: Considered equivalent to "dangerous mechanism" by most clinical guidelines
- **Anticoagulated patients**: Rule does NOT apply — CT should be obtained regardless

## CT Scan Protocol
- **Non-contrast CT head**: Standard for acute trauma
- **CT angiography**: Add if vascular injury suspected (e.g., skull base fracture)
- **Window settings**: Review on both brain and bone windows

## What to Look For
- **Epidural hematoma**: Biconvex/lenticular hyperdensity, typically temporal, often arterial
- **Subdural hematoma**: Crescent-shaped hyperdensity, can be bilateral, often venous
- **Subarachnoid hemorrhage**: Hyperdensity in sulci and cisterns
- **Intracerebral hemorrhage/contusion**: Hyperdense focus within brain parenchyma
- **Skull fracture**: Bone window — linear or depressed
- **Midline shift**: > 5mm is concerning for herniation
- **Cerebral edema**: Loss of grey-white differentiation, sulcal effacement

## Urgency
- **IMMEDIATE (within 1 hour)**: GCS ≤ 12, deteriorating GCS, focal neurological deficit, anticoagulated patient
- **URGENT (within 8 hours)**: GCS 13–15 with any CCHR criterion met
- **NOT indicated**: GCS 15, no CCHR criteria met, no anticoagulation
