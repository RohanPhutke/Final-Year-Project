# Chest X-Ray — Rib Fracture & Thoracic Trauma Assessment

## Overview
Lateral falls frequently result in rib fractures, particularly in elderly or osteoporotic patients. While isolated rib fractures are generally benign, complications such as pneumothorax, hemothorax, and flail chest can be life-threatening.

## When to Request Chest X-Ray After a Fall
- Direct impact to the chest wall or lateral thorax
- Rib tenderness or crepitus on palpation
- Pleuritic chest pain (worse with breathing)
- Dyspnea or tachypnea
- Reduced oxygen saturation
- Any lateral fall with high-energy impact
- Elderly patient (≥65) with any chest wall pain after fall

## Standard Views
1. **PA (Posteroanterior)**: Standard upright view — best for pneumothorax, effusions
2. **Lateral**: Helps localize pathology and detect posterior rib fractures
3. **Expiratory film**: Enhances visualization of small pneumothoraces (if suspected but not seen on standard PA)

## What to Look For

### Rib Fractures
- Cortical disruption or lucent line through rib
- Note: UP TO 50% of rib fractures are not visible on initial chest X-ray
- **Rib detail views** are no longer routinely recommended (they don't change management)
- Count the number and location of fractures:
  - **Upper ribs (1–3)**: High-energy mechanism; check for vascular injury (aorta, subclavian)
  - **Middle ribs (4–9)**: Most common; check for pneumothorax
  - **Lower ribs (10–12)**: Check for liver (right) or splenic (left) injury

### Complications to Identify
- **Pneumothorax**: Visceral pleural line, absence of lung markings peripherally
- **Hemothorax**: Blunting of costophrenic angle, opacification of hemithorax
- **Flail chest**: ≥ 3 adjacent ribs fractured in ≥ 2 places — paradoxical chest wall movement
- **Pulmonary contusion**: Patchy opacification (may take 6–24 hours to develop)
- **Subcutaneous emphysema**: Air tracking in soft tissues (visible on X-ray and palpable as crepitus)

## Severity Assessment

### Low Risk (Outpatient management possible)
- 1–2 isolated rib fractures (ribs 4–9)
- No pneumothorax or hemothorax
- Adequate pain control
- SpO2 > 95% on room air
- Age < 65

### High Risk (Admission required)
- ≥ 3 rib fractures
- Flail segment
- Any pneumothorax or hemothorax
- Age ≥ 65 (mortality doubles with each additional rib fracture in elderly)
- Pre-existing lung disease
- Anticoagulant therapy

## Advanced Imaging
- **CT Chest**: If high clinical suspicion despite normal X-ray, or to quantify injury burden
- **Ultrasound (eFAST)**: Rapid bedside detection of pneumothorax and hemothorax — more sensitive than X-ray

## Urgency
- **STAT**: Respiratory distress, suspected tension pneumothorax, hemodynamic instability
- **URGENT (within 1 hour)**: Chest wall tenderness with dyspnea, reduced SpO2
- **ROUTINE**: Chest wall pain, no respiratory compromise
