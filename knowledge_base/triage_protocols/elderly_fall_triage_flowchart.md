# Elderly Fall Triage Flowchart

## Overview
Falls are the leading cause of injury-related death in adults aged 65+. Elderly patients frequently present with atypical symptoms and may have significant injuries with minimal complaints. A systematic approach is essential.

## Initial Assessment Flowchart

### Step 1: Scene Safety & Mechanism
- Is the patient in a dangerous location? → Move only if necessary
- Determine mechanism: trip, slip, syncope, mechanical fall, or seizure-related
- Assess fall height and surface: carpet, tile, concrete, stairs
- Witnesses present? Duration on ground?

### Step 2: Primary Survey (ABCDE)
- Follow standard ABCDE approach
- Pay special attention to:
  - Anticoagulant medication (bleeding risk significantly elevated)
  - Pacemaker/ICD presence
  - Baseline cognitive status (dementia patients may not report symptoms)

### Step 3: Secondary Survey — Head to Toe
- **Head**: Scalp lacerations, haematomas, Battle sign, raccoon eyes
- **C-spine**: Midline tenderness, neurological deficit
- **Chest**: Rib tenderness, crepitus, reduced air entry
- **Abdomen**: Tenderness (splenic injury possible even in low-energy falls in anticoagulated patients)
- **Pelvis**: Lateral compression test (gentle), symphysis tenderness
- **Limbs**: Shortening, rotation, swelling, deformity, distal pulses
- **Skin**: Pressure areas (long-lie), skin tears, bruising patterns

### Step 4: Determine the Cause of the Fall
- **Mechanical**: Tripped, slipped — focus on injury management
- **Syncope**: Cardiac arrhythmia, postural hypotension, vasovagal — needs cardiac monitoring
- **Neurological**: Stroke, TIA, seizure — rapid neurological assessment, time-critical
- **Medication-related**: Polypharmacy, new medications, benzodiazepines

## Long-Lie Assessment
If the patient was on the ground for > 1 hour:
- Check for **rhabdomyolysis**: CK levels, urine colour
- Check for **hypothermia**: core temperature
- Check for **pressure injuries**: sacrum, heels, occiput
- Check for **dehydration**: mucous membranes, skin turgor
- Check for **DVT/PE risk**: immobilization period

## Triage Priority
- **RED**: Hemodynamic instability, GCS < 13, suspected spinal injury, active hemorrhage
- **YELLOW**: Hip/long bone fracture, significant pain, syncope cause unclear, anticoagulated patient with head injury
- **GREEN**: Minor soft tissue injury, ambulatory, no red flags

## Key Elderly-Specific Considerations
- Vital signs may be "normal" despite significant injury (beta-blockers mask tachycardia)
- Assume cervical spine injury with any head injury in osteoporotic patients
- Low threshold for imaging — clinical examination alone is unreliable
- Social assessment: can they mobilize at home? Is this a recurrent fall?
